Charging Guide

Complete EV Charging Guide
for UK Drivers 2026

✍️ Priya Sharma 📅 3 June 2026 ⏱️ 12 min read 🔄 Updated June 2026

At a glance: Most UK EV drivers do 80–90% of their charging at home overnight, adding around 200 miles for less than £4. A 7kW home charger is the right starting point for most households. Public rapid charging is available for longer journeys but costs 3–5× more per mile than home charging.

Charging is the biggest concern for people considering their first electric vehicle. But in practice, it's far simpler than most people expect — especially if you can charge at home. This guide covers everything you need to know, from choosing a home charger to navigating the UK's public charging networks on long journeys.

Part 1: Home Charging

Why Home Charging Is the Foundation

The single biggest advantage of owning an EV is waking up every morning to a full battery. If you drive less than 200 miles per day — which is 99.9% of UK drivers — you can complete every ordinary journey on overnight home charging alone. Public charging becomes a tool for occasional longer trips, not a daily necessity.

Charger Types Explained

3-pin plug (2.3kW): Every EV comes with a 3-pin plug cable for emergency use. At 2.3kW, it adds roughly 8 miles per hour of charging. It'll fully charge a small EV (like the Renault 5) overnight, but it's slow for larger batteries and puts stress on a standard socket. We don't recommend it as your primary home charging method.

7kW home charger (single-phase): This is the sweet spot for most UK homes. A 7kW wallbox adds around 30 miles per hour of charging, meaning you'll fully charge most EVs in 6–10 hours overnight. Installation costs £600–900 in total (hardware + fitting), minus the £350 OZEV grant.

22kW home charger (three-phase): Only suitable for homes with three-phase electricity supply (rare in UK domestic properties). Adds around 90 miles per hour. Most drivers don't need this — the 7kW is sufficient overnight.

The OZEV Home Charger Grant

The Office for Zero Emission Vehicles (OZEV) offers up to £350 off the purchase and installation of a home EV charger. To qualify in 2026:

  • You must own or have a conditional sale agreement for an eligible EV or PHEV
  • You must own or rent the property where the charger will be installed
  • The charger must be installed by an OZEV-approved installer
  • Renters and flat owners can now apply (the grant was extended in 2023)

The grant is applied automatically by the installer — you don't need to claim it separately. Just make sure to use an OZEV-approved provider (all major charger brands qualify).

Best Home Chargers for UK Buyers in 2026

Ohme Home Pro (7kW): Our top pick. Smart scheduling, excellent app, works with Intelligent Octopus and other smart tariffs. Automatically charges when electricity is cheapest. £799 installed (after OZEV grant: ~£449).

Pod Point Solo 3 (7kW): The UK's most popular home charger for a reason — reliable, simple, and backed by an extensive installer network. Good choice if you want a no-fuss installation. £749 installed (after OZEV grant: ~£399).

Part 2: Public Charging

Understanding Charge Speeds

Public chargers range from painfully slow to impressively fast. Here's how to understand the numbers:

  • Slow (3–7kW): Found at car parks, lamp posts, and older installations. Adds 10–30 miles/hour. Fine for a 3-hour supermarket shop, useless for a motorway stop.
  • Fast (11–22kW): Common at retail destinations and hotels. Adds 50–90 miles/hour. Good for an hour-long visit or overnight hotel stop.
  • Rapid (50–100kW): Motorway service stations and major hubs. Adds 80–200 miles in 30–45 minutes. The workhorse of long-distance EV travel.
  • Ultra-rapid (150–350kW+): Available on the Gridserve, Tesla, and Osprey networks. Adds 100+ miles in 10–20 minutes (if your car supports it).

The Main UK Public Charging Networks

Tesla Supercharger: Now open to non-Tesla EVs at most UK locations. Over 700 Supercharger sites in the UK, with average speeds of 100–250kW. Consistently the most reliable network we've tested. Pay per kWh via the Tesla app (no subscription needed).

Gridserve Electric Highway: The best non-Tesla rapid charging network in the UK. 300kW chargers at motorway services, pay-as-you-go with contactless card payment. Excellent reliability track record in 2025–2026.

BP Pulse: The largest network by number of points, but quality varies. Their rapid hubs at motorway services are good. Older slow chargers at supermarkets are inconsistent. Subscription available for heavy users.

Pod Point: Reliable slow and fast chargers at ASDA, IKEA, and other retail sites. Generally free or low cost. Good for topping up during shopping.

Osprey: Smaller network but high-quality 150kW+ chargers in well-chosen locations. Pay by contactless card, no app needed.

How to Plan a Long Journey

The easiest approach for UK long-distance driving in 2026 is to plan charging stops using your car's built-in navigation (Tesla, Rivian, Polestar, and most new EVs route to chargers automatically). For other cars, the A Better Route Planner (ABRP) app is the best third-party option and is free to use.

A practical tip: on a motorway journey, aim to arrive at a charger with 15–20% battery remaining and charge to around 80%. Above 80%, most EVs slow their charging rate significantly. Two shorter stops are often faster than one full charge.

Part 3: Charging Costs

Charging costs depend heavily on whether you're charging at home or on the public network, and which electricity tariff you're on:

  • Home (standard tariff, ~25p/kWh): ~£4.50 for a full charge of a 60kWh battery (200+ miles). About 2p per mile.
  • Home (Intelligent Octopus tariff, ~7p/kWh overnight): ~£1.30 for a full charge of a 60kWh battery. About 0.6p per mile — cheaper than cycling.
  • Public rapid (Gridserve, ~65p/kWh): ~£39 for a full charge. About 15p per mile — still cheaper than petrol at current prices, but not the huge saving of home charging.

Smart Tariff Tip: Switching to a smart EV electricity tariff (Intelligent Octopus, Ovo EV Everywhere, or OVO Beyond) with a smart home charger can reduce your home charging cost by 70% compared to standard rates. The Ohme charger and Intelligent Octopus tariff combination is our recommended setup for UK drivers.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I charge in the rain? Yes. All EV charging equipment sold in the UK is rated for outdoor, wet-weather use. It is safe to plug in and unplug in rain — the contacts are shielded and the socket locks when live.

What happens if a public charger is broken? Use a real-time availability app like Zap-Map or your car's navigation to find an alternative before you arrive. Most areas have multiple networks within a few miles of motorway services. Carry a CHAdeMO or Type 1 adapter if your car supports multiple connector standards.

Do I need to pay for charging at a Tesla Supercharger if I don't own a Tesla? Yes — the free Supercharging perk is only for Tesla owners who received it as part of their purchase deal. All other vehicles (including non-Tesla EVs using the Magic Dock CCS adapter) pay per kWh at standard rates via the Tesla app.
